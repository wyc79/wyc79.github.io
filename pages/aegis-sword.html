Why

I wanted to practice translating a stylized game prop into a clean, manufacturable CAD model‚Äîespecially handling asymmetry, layered details, and tight proportion matching from reference.

This was my final project for EAS 143: Introduction to Solid Modeling (University of Rochester, Spring 2021), where the goal was to build accurate parts and assemblies using parametric workflows.

What

A detailed 3D CAD replication of the Aegis Sword from Xenoblade Chronicles 2, modeled in Onshape with an emphasis on readable silhouette, precise detailing, and assembly-ready structure.

How

Workflow

  • Reference breakdown: Collected screenshots and orthographic-ish angles, then identified key proportions (blade length, guard width, handle thickness) to lock the silhouette early.
  • Modular parts: Split the sword into repeatable subcomponents (blade core, guard, handle, emblem/ornament), so edits wouldn‚Äôt cascade across the whole model.
  • Parametric feature tree: Used sketches + extrudes/fillets/patterns with named dimensions to keep the model editable during iteration.

Design & detailing

  • Asymmetry handling: Established a consistent datum setup and only mirrored where the design truly repeats; the rest was modeled intentionally off-axis to match the original.
  • Clean edges & readability: Tuned bevels/fillets to avoid ‚Äútoo sharp‚Äù transitions while keeping crisp highlights on the main forms.
  • Assembly mindset: Modeled as an assembly with part separation in mind (useful for future export, printing, or game-ready retopology workflows).

Result

The final model reproduces the sword‚Äôs layered structure and distinctive asymmetry, and the modular setup made it straightforward to refine proportions and surface details late in the process.
