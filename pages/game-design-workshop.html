Up the River: Variation / Game Mod

Why

Build a variation on a shared base game (Up the River) by changing constraints and mechanics, then iterate through structured playtests while keeping a specific experience goal in focus.

What

Our variation reworked the original linear track into a circular race-to-treasure loop with environmental tiles (Wave/Reef), Pirate hazards, and a card system that lets players speed up their ship or hinder others. The intended experience was pressure + strategy as players chase points while avoiding danger.

How (weekly iteration)

Week 1 ‚Äî Core loop + first pass balancing (9/21‚Äì9/23)
  • Defined the core loop: travel between Port and Treasure Island on a circular track, using dice + event cards to create risk/reward decisions.
  • Iterated quickly on pacing and probability: expanded the board, switched from D4 to D6, increased pirate pressure, and tightened card/rule wording so turns stayed snappy (we focused on pacing/balance tuning + clarity).
Week 2 ‚Äî Playtest-driven clarity + V2 polish (9/24‚Äì9/30)
  • Ran a lab playtest and rewrote setup/turn order text for clarity; tuned incentives (e.g., when card draws trigger) to reduce ‚Äúdead‚Äù rolls.
  • Conducted internal playtests to reduce downtime and edge-case confusion (reshuffle rules, clearer card effects, max hand size), then consolidated everything into a cleaner V2 with a faster, smoother flow (we led setup/turn-order + edge-case clarity fixes and helped consolidate the final V2 rules).

This project was selected for in-class presentation.

Mechanics Project: Focused Mechanic Exploration

Why

Practice mechanics-first design: choose a core interaction, prototype fast, and use formal feedback to refine clarity, pacing, and player interpretation.

This project started from randomly drawn topics. Our team selected four key words ‚Äî auction, versus, romance, and solution-finding ‚Äî and our goal was to turn them into a playable game.

We specifically wanted to explore how auctions can function as both a competitive system and a narrative device, letting emotional stakes emerge from mechanical choices (instead of scripted story beats).

What

Too Bird To Handle is a silent-coordination bidding game themed around birds courting. Players coordinate without speaking, trying to read intention and manage risk through a ‚Äúgive or take‚Äù style action loop.

Built around an auction-style interaction, the experience emphasizes reading intent, managing risk, and negotiating value under social pressure.

How (weekly iteration)

Week 1 ‚Äî Prompt ‚Üí playable prototype + first external tests (Week 9)
  • Used a ‚Äúyes-and‚Äù brainstorm to interpret the prompt, then committed to an auction-inspired, four-player cooperative direction about birds searching for mates.
  • Built a minimum playable prototype: sketched resource and bird cards, established clear win/loss conditions, and iterated internally to smooth the difficulty curve (less swingy, more teamwork).
  • Ran early external playtests and captured the biggest issues: players misread the game as competitive, terminology (‚Äúbid/bird‚Äù) was confusing, and setup/visibility needed cleaner structure.
  • Turned playtest feedback into concrete fixes (terminology, setup clarity, table layout), and tightened win/loss wording for faster onboarding.
Week 2 ‚Äî Clarity pass + visual polish + finalization (Week 10)
  • Ran additional external playtests that confirmed stronger engagement and pacing, while still highlighting edge-case questions and moments where the rules needed explicit guidance.
  • Validated changes through internal playtests, then did a final consistency/clarity pass across the rulebook, cards, and board to remove edge cases and improve readability (so the experience matched the intended ‚Äúsilent teamwork‚Äù tension).

This project was selected for in-class presentation.

Final Project: Original Team Game; Role: Producer

Why

The final project is a free project: define an original experience goal, build an end-to-end prototype, and demonstrate playcentric iteration across multiple weeks (rules, components, balance, and presentation).

What

Our team built a competitive survival game centered on social hierarchy. Players take on different roles (upper- to lower-class) and must meet role-specific needs inspired by Maslow‚Äôs hierarchy while dealing with threats, deception, and negotiation as resources flow from top to bottom each round.

How (weekly iteration)

  • Week 1 ‚Äî Core loop + first playable: We defined the concept, scope, and primary mechanics (roles + descending resources), aligned on the intended player experience, and built an early prototype for quick internal/external playtests. We helped lock the core loop and tightened the threat / gun rules based on early feedback.
  • Week 2 ‚Äî Clarity + agency pass: We expanded and stabilized systems through playtesting, then improved readability and decision-making: ‚Äúpet‚Äù ‚Üí love, shifted to a single-winner victory condition, increased lower-class agency (starting hand), and refined the rulebook for fewer edge-case questions. We drove rule/wording cleanup and helped implement the agency + win-condition revisions.
  • Week 3 ‚Äî Balance with simulation support: We ran a focused balance pass supported by 200k-trial simulations, adjusted deck composition, and updated win conditions to reduce ‚Äúaccidental‚Äù wins, then validated changes via final playtests. We ran the simulation-backed balance checks and translated results into concrete deck/win-condition updates.
  • Week 4 ‚Äî Presentation polish: Finalized the slide deck and recorded the final presentation video. We supported deck polish and final deliverable prep.
